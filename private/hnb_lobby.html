Huntington Digital Lobby

Project Overview

In my time working at Huntington National Bank, I was fully dedicated to the conception and launch of a new to bank application that supports the sales and onboarding of business and commercial customers, including prospective customers, to the bank. As part of this platform, a key feature defined was the need for an in-application messaging solution, as a means to help replace email communication happening between customers and Huntington colleagues today.

As the lead interaction designer on the project, I was responsible for collaborating across multiple areas of the business, between product partners, process SMEs, architecture, and various technical teams, to design a viable solution that would meet the needs of the business and its customers alike. Here's how we did it.

Understanding the Problem

A need was identified in the commercial and business banking areas of the bank to create a more seamless and efficient onboarding process for prospective customers to open and action on their accounts. The process at the time was filled with pain points around miscommunmications, duplicate document requests, and an overall lack of clarity.

A view of the application at the start of our work

Prospect Login

By this point in the maturity of our design team, we had established a cadence of "understanding" sessions with business and product partners to help narrow our focus on the story of each upcoming feature, their goals as the business, the needs for our customers, as well as any assumptions we might be making. After dedicating the entirety of one of these sessions to this new feature, I walked away with a good grasp of what the business was imagining for this capability, and immediately set out on collecting as many examples of online messaging systems that I could find. Following this exercise, I documented the commonalities we saw across the current Huntington ecosystem, as well as with competitors and non-banking experiences alike, and came away with ideas on the many possible paths forward.

Document Collection

With so many directions to choose from, it was important to get my bearings on exactly everything we wanted to be possible with this messaging solution. I started by listing out the high-level use cases and scenarios that both colleagues and customers could experience using such a solution, and validated with the business which were relevant for our first iteration, and those that could be saved for future versions. From there, I explored the various entry point and navigation models that could result from adding messaging into our existing application, and listed out the pros and cons associated with each, taking those into conversations with the business to help further align and narrow down our approach. While these conversations and decisions were in flux, I continued to explore where I could, putting together object models and concepts that fit within the parameters we had defined to that point.

Onboarding Tasks

After creating and aligning on initial concepts, we took our ideas to our technical and architecture partners to discuss feasibility and constraints. Because the backend system was powered by Salesforce, it was important to use a solution and framework that our colleagues would use and understand, while not requiring a lot of custom coding or overly intricate technical design. With their expertise and support, the team was able to align on a few possible approaches, and after thoroughly vetting each, landed on a solution using a similar framework developed for our "Tasks" area of the application. This solution would help ensure familiarity with the interface and area of Salesforce impacted, while meeting the needs and goals of what we had defined thus far.

Our defined "approaches" that were used to help inform the tech design

Enter Lending

With the backend figured out, we were now able to narrow down some of the more minute details of the interactions and behaviors of the customer-facing experience. Over the course of many collaborative discussions involving BSAs, technical leads, and product partners, we were able to determine specifics such as message character limits, conversation states and statuses, retention limits, and more. And when we arrived at a place where we all felt comfortable moving forward, it was time to finally test the solution with users.

Working with the researchers on my design team, we developed a rapid usability study using UserTesting.com to help understand where users may run into pain points with the experience. I created a highly interactive prototype of the interface, and helped narrow down the question set to the areas we wished to focus on. We launched the test immediately after returning from our holiday break, and upon listening to the results, we found that our only major area of concern was around the "archive" functionality. What we learned was that users interpreted the word "archive" differently from how we were presenting it, hoping that it would save their messages for later rather than as a way of removing messages and organizing their main inbox. To mitigate this, we regrouped as a team and quickly pivoted to a secondary concept of "hiding" message conversations, which allowed for the same functionality as archiving without the confusion on what would occur when enacted. While the timing of the project didn't allow for a follow up test, the design team as well as our product partners felt confident in the clarity of this new direction.

Notifications

With the technical specifics determined and user feedback addressed, all that was left was to create the final deliverable and specifications for our development partners. Though I was primarily responsible for creating the wireframes and subsequent annotations, it was also my responsibility to help communicate my designs to the various stakeholders and teams impacted by this feature, including to our internal design leaders as well as various business SMEs and representatives. As the wireframes were finalized, I additionally helped coordinate and assist with the creation of our visual and content design deliverables, ensuring a consistent direction for both in alignment with my wireframes.
